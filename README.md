1. Introduction
This report documents my work completed as part of the CSE0001 – Digital Literacy project at VIT Bhopal University. The scenario for this project places me in the role of a Student Digital Ambassador, responsible for helping my peers navigate the digital world effectively and safely.
Over the course of five tasks, I explored core aspects of digital literacy: creating an awareness infographic, building a professional online portfolio, practising on coding and collaboration platforms, drafting professional emails, and researching cybercrime threats faced by college students in India. Each task corresponds to one module of the course and contributes to a single GitHub repository, which serves as my Digital Literacy Portfolio. This report presents a structured overview of each task, the tools and platforms I used, my key observations, and personal reflections on what I learned.
2. Task 1 – Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic
For Task 1, I created a one-page digital literacy awareness infographic using Canva (canva.com), a free browser-based design tool. I chose Canva because it offers a wide range of pre-designed templates and drag-and-drop functionality that made it easy to work with even without prior design experience.
My infographic covers three key topics: (1) What Digital Literacy means and why it matters for students today, (2) Safe internet practices including password hygiene and avoiding phishing links, and (3) Professional online presence — why platforms like LinkedIn and GitHub matter early in a student's career. I used a clean, colour-coded layout with icons and short bullet points to make the information easy to read at a glance.
One aspect I found challenging was choosing what to leave out. There is so much to say about digital literacy that fitting everything onto a single page required real editorial decisions. However, this exercise taught me the value of clarity and conciseness in visual communication — skills that are just as important as the content itself. The exported infographic is saved in the task-1-presentation/ folder of the repository.

5. Task 4 – Professional Email & Etiquette Guide
For Task 4, I drafted two professional emails and created a social media etiquette checklist. Email 1 was addressed to a professor requesting a three-day extension on an assignment submission. Email 2 expressed interest in a summer internship to a company coordinator. Both emails follow professional structure: a clear subject line, appropriate salutation, well-organised body paragraphs, and a courteous sign-off with full contact details.
To illustrate the real consequences of poor digital communication, consider this scenario: a student, frustrated with a group project, posts a strongly worded complaint on a public Facebook group naming their professor and institution. Within hours, the post is screenshot and shared widely. The student faces disciplinary action and the post surfaces in future employer searches for their name. The situation could have been entirely avoided by raising the concern privately via email, using a respectful and professional tone. This scenario reinforced for me that the internet has no "undo" button — every post is a permanent professional decision.
6. Task 5 – Cybercrime Awareness Case Study & Prevention Guide
For Task 5, I chose UPI/Online Payment Fraud as my cybercrime type, given its direct relevance to college students in India who are increasingly using digital payment platforms. My case study describes how fraudsters create a false sense of urgency, ask victims to install screen-sharing apps, and trick them into approving COLLECT REQUESTS — which debit money from the victim's account, not credit it. The full case study is saved in task-5-cybercrime/casestudy.md.
What surprised me most during my research was the psychological sophistication of these scams. Fraudsters are not just technically skilled — they are expert manipulators who exploit trust, urgency, and generosity. I had assumed cybercrime primarily targeted the elderly or less educated, but the data shows that educated, tech-savvy young people are frequently victimised precisely because they are overconfident in their digital knowledge.
The one habit I will personally change as a result of this research is to never approve a UPI request without first verifying the purpose and the sender independently — not through the same phone call or message that prompted the request. I also intend to share the prevention checklist with my family members, especially those less familiar with digital payments.
7. Conclusion
This project has been one of the most practically valuable assignments I have completed so far in my academic journey. Prior to this project, I had accounts on some platforms but had never thought strategically about how they connect to form a professional digital identity. Working through each of the five tasks gave me both technical skills — setting up repositories, building forms, completing coding challenges — and softer but equally important skills: writing professionally, communicating with empathy, and thinking critically about my online presence and safety. I now understand that digital literacy is not a technical skill but a life skill, and I look forward to building on this foundation throughout my four years at VIT Bhopal.
